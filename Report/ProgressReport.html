Progress Report

Our approach to The 2110 Portal

The 2110 Portal began with the idea to highlight workplaces within United Auto Workers Union (UAW) Local 2110, as it is an influential union within the New York City area that represents workers from over 40 workplaces and spans multiple industries and types of cultural institutions. (View UAW 2110’s list of workplaces here.) UAW Local 2110 has been involved in many recent unionization efforts within the city, some influenced by the pandemic, and all by the various needs of today’s workforce. Our website hopes to answer the question of why cultural institutions seek unionization and how demands of different industries within Local 2110 compare.

As we began the process of creating our first interactive digital piece of journalism, we based much of our thinking off of the key takeaways in the MIT Open Documentary Lab report, “Mapping the Intersection of Two Cultures: Interactive Documentary and Digital Journalism.” The takeaways that influenced our project most were the following:


Let story determine form (Urrichio, 6)

We wanted our website to be as engaging on a human and emotional level as possible. Unionization is at its heart the standing together of a group of people. Although there is much data associated with this story, especially when analyzing union agreements, we wanted the user to come away with an experience of the people who are making advancement of workplace rights possible. To do so, we decided to embed audio clips of our interviewees throughout the narrative on our website, in hopes of actively reminding the reader who they are reading about as they consume the information.


Experiment and learn (Urrichio, 6)

The most time-consuming aspect of this project was learning how to use interactive tools to accomplish our goals. We mainly used Twine, Soundcite, and StoryMap, but we also had a learning curve implementing certain aspects of HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. However, now that we have gone through the process of making this project, it will be much easier to incorporate these skills into future journalistic endeavors.


Shape conversations and Consider long-term impact (Urrichio, 6)

UAW Local 2110 covers many industries, yet their site does not sort workplaces by industry or need. As seen in unionization trends throughout the past few years, when employees at one workplace accomplish a goal like getting higher pay or receiving health insurance, there is often a trickle effect once other workplaces learn of what is accomplished. We wanted to provide a source that breaks down needs and gives insight into the unionization process by industry. The hope is that those who are looking to unionize in their workplace, or even those who are not, will be able to read about the needs and accomplishments of their industry and enact change in their own workplace in an informed manner. We also hope this site makes unionization seem less intimidating and more possible for users to implement in their own lives, grounded in the human experience as it is.


Our process creating The 2110 Portal

Crafting a Story

The creation of our storyboard and question of exploration gave us a clear roadmap for site creation. We decided that our question of exploration would be “How do union agreements with UAW Local 2110 compare between industries?”, and would serve as a North Star for our content. Outlining the industries we would explore, and deciding which union member we would target, we established subjects for our investigation into employees’ fight for an equitable workforce and the journey they’ve taken toward it. Ith the blueprint in-hand, e built a Twine, structuring it by the page outlined in the image above: Chapter I: Intro, Chapter II: The Pandemic’s Effects, Chapter III: The Arts Industry, Chapter IV: Academia, Chapter V: The Media Industry, and Chapter VI: Predictions + Conclusions.

Next we filled in the pages through website copy and field research. The initial drafted copy for each Twine page, which is accessible via this link, added context and background to each industry's agreements. Through a literature review of articles and documents, we accumulated a wealth of contextual data that sheds light on the macroeconomic and health trendss that affected union needs, progress, and often times, were the impetuses for employer abuses. Scheduling interviews and site visits to our workplaces of interest, we began gathering media and intellectual content. Before each interview, we prepped a list of questions whose answers would provide valuable information for the comparison of agreements across industries. The objective of doing so was that the content, extracted from interviews, that’s quoted and used throughout the website either answers our question of exploration indirectly or gives grounds for answering it.

Collecting Raw Material and Soundcite Audio Clips

We began the process of collecting audio by researching the workplaces and industries represented by UAW Local 2110 and contacting union representatives or workers who had recently unionized. We decided to focus on recently unionized workplaces to highlight the current trends and needs of workers. Audio interviews were conducted with workers from the New Museum of Contemporary Art, Barnard Contingent Faculty, and HarperCollins Publishers to represent the arts industry, academia, and the media industry respectively. Topics of interest included motivation to unionize, process and outcomes of unionization, remaining needs, and changes to be made in the larger industry.

These interviews were edited into clips and published to Internet Archive, and the program Soundcite was used to create code for inline audio players for each clip. The clips were incorporated into articles on each industry’s page on Twine for the user to listen to while reading about the industry.

Videos and photos of each workplace were collected and incorporated into the Twine pages of each industry to help place the reader in the location.

Collecting Workplace Data and Coding the Website

Once we narrowed down our workplaces and industries of focus, we began gathering information about their union agreements, available on UAW 2110’s website. We started with lists of the union articles in the first and most recent union agreement(s) of each workplace, and kept track of the most popular union articles. The data spreadsheet was used to find the exact data of most common union articles among all workplace union contracts, and this data was used within the interactive graph on the Twine to define the union articles and provide more clarity about what goes into a union agreement. Data regarding each workplaces’ union agreements was also included within Storymaps of all 2110 workplaces, and the workplaces by our industries of focus. The Storymaps were integrated into the Twine, the main aspect of our site, so that users of our site can visualize the workplace locations across the city while also having access to links to the union agreements and the workplaces’ websites.

While the Twine was the main aspect of our homepage, we made several other pages to go into our site menu, including the Twine on its own page, the javascript quiz, this progress report, and our bibliography. We tried various methods of embedding our Twine and other interactive elements on our site to find the best format for storytelling, but most elements were integrated into the Twine to create a singular, continuous story within our home page.

Bringing the Story to Life Visually

With our raw content gathered, interactive content embedded, website copy coded, and original content collected, the next step was to supplement what we already had visually in order to bring the stories of the Workplaces, our interviewees, and the 2110 union to life in the minds of readers. We began retrieving and downloading archival content, logging everything in this spreadsheet, and embedding subsequent content into the site with proper citations. Our objective was to find images that offer a different perspective, physically, visually, and occasionally emotionally, of the Workplaces in discussion. The thoughtful placement of the image sets tones we weren’t able to adequately capture in our site visit content. The archival content anchors the stories and gives readers a better sense of pace and time as the employee feels them.

Next, we changed font sizes as needed to create emphasis and improve how the site’s text reads. Certain key words and ideas headline interview text and reflections and were made to catch the reader’s eye before having all necessary context. We also created Flourish charts, interactive data visualizations, to improve how the data is digested by readers and add some dynamism to stagnant text-heavy portions of the site.
